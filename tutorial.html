What is 3D Printing?

3D Printing, also known as additive manufacturing, is a method of creating three dimensional objects by depositing thin layers of material until a 3D part is formed. If you want to learn more about the technical aspects of 3D printing, consider going here or joining the Penn State 3D printing club here!

1. Choosing Your Print

In order to select your print, simply navigate to the main page of the website and select the model you would like to download. Once on the individual virus page, select the .makerbot download to download your file. If the model you are downloading is an assembly, you may receive a zipped folder. Simply right click this folder once downloaded and selected “Extract All” to receive your files.

Figure 1: Downloading the Model

NOTE: When choosing your model, you may want to think about how easy the model is to print and finalize. Models with small nooks and crannies, or large amounts of overhangs and small pieces will likely include a large amount of support material that may be difficult to reach or remove. Complex models may take an hour or more to remove supports without breaking the model itself. If this is your first time 3D printing, it is advisable to choose a model that is rather smooth and a simple shape, such a spherical model (ex. PhiX174) as these will likely have less frustrating support materials to work with. If you would like a better sense of what your final model will look like, and predict where supports may appear, download and open the .stl file located next to the .makerbot file of your print to view the base 3D model. See more about removing support in the Finalizing your print section of this guide.

2. Uploading Your Print

Once you have downloaded the model you wish to print, navigate to the Penn State’s Maker Commons submission site at https://makercommons.psu.edu/3dsubmit/ and select whether your print is personal or for a class. Next you will be prompted to upload your .makerbot file to the checkup tool to verify that it will print. If any of the categories come back as FAIL please contact your instructor or email jxk5760@psu.edu. Next fill out the submission form and submit, then follow the link to Penn State’s MakerBot Innovation Center https://psu.innovationcenter.makerbot.com/.

Figure 2: Checkup Tool

Figure 3: Submission Form

If this is your first time using the Maker Commons, you will have to make an account using your Penn State email. Once signed in, navigate to “New Print” on the left-hand side. Enter the name of your project. Do not select a deadline, as it is unable to be enforced. Under “Filament Information” select “PLA”, then select the color you would like your print to be. Next ensure that you have “Upload Files” selected then uploaded the same .makerbot file here that you uploaded to the checkup tool. Keep in mind that although multiple filetypes are listed as acceptable, only .makerbot files that passed the checkup tool will be approved for printing. Finally, if you are at any Penn State campus other than University Park, enter your campus in the notes section at the bottom to have the print shipped to your campus’ library. Once you have completed these steps, hit next then confirm your print. If you need multiple models, or have multiple parts to a print, you must resubmit each file individually to the innovation center, as single requests with multiple models may not be honored.

3. Picking Up Your Print

Once your submitted prints have been approved by the maker commons, you will receive an email indicated so. Once approved, prints may take anywhere from a few hours to a week to be printed, depending on how busy the maker commons is. Keep in mind that the maker commons is generally busiest near the end of the semester, when many student need 3D models for final projects, and may restrict submissions to those required by a class. If you are at a commonwealth campus, expect an extra 3-5 days for your print to be shipped. If you are at university park, go to the common services desk on the first floor of the Pattee/Paterno Library to pick up your print. If you are at a commonwealth campus, pick up your print from your campuses library.

4. Finalizing Your Print

Once you receive your print, you may notice there is a lot of extra material attached to it. This is called support material, and is used to allow a 3D printer to print over what would otherwise be empty space. This support can be identified by often being very choppy and existing under areas of your model that would otherwise hang over empty space. The solid layer of support on the bottom, called the raft, helps keep the model securely on the bed while printing, and can often be easily removed by hand. For removing the rest of the support, while some can be removed by hand, it is generally advised to use tools to assist you. Tools such as a flathead screwdriver, wire cutters, and plyers generally work very well for removing support in small or tough to reach areas. Be very careful not to break off other parts of the model while removing support, or you may have to start over and reprint the entire model! If you need visual assistance while removing support, it may be a good idea to download and open the .stl file located on the same page as the .makerbot files in order to view what your final model should look like and give you a visual reference. Once the support is removed, you will now have your final print!
